# Employee Attrition Analysis — Power BI Dashboard

237 employees left last year. $24.73M in replacement costs.
And the company's response was to call it an HR problem.
This project argues otherwise.

## Why I Built This

16.12% attrition. Above every industry benchmark.
That number kept showing up in reports and nobody was 
really asking — okay but who is leaving, why, and 
what is it actually costing us?

So I built something that tries to answer that.
Five pages. One dataset. 1,470 employees.

## What Each Page Is Actually Doing

**Page 1 — Command Centre**  
The big picture. Attrition rate, flight risk headcount, 
regrettable exits, total cost. The kind of slide a CEO 
looks at for 90 seconds and either acts on or doesn't.
I wanted those 90 seconds to count.

**Page 2 — Workforce Risk**  
Not everyone is equally likely to leave. New hires are 
walking out at 34.88%. Gen Z at 28.10%. People doing 
overtime — 30.53% gone, vs 10.4% without it.
The risk is concentrated. That's actually useful to know.

**Page 3 — Exit Drivers**  
This one bothered me the most honestly.
People aren't leaving for money. Career growth is #1 
at 27.7% of exits. And leavers scored 32.89 on engagement 
vs 57.31 for people who stayed. 42 points. That gap 
doesn't appear overnight — someone missed the signals 
for months, maybe longer.

**Page 4 — Manager Impact**  
Every single rising performer who left — regrettable. 
100%. First year with a new manager is the most 
dangerous — 28.32% attrition. And 43% of the workforce 
is already sitting in teams above the danger threshold.
At some point this stops being a coincidence.

**Page 5 — Financial Impact**  
Average cost per exit is $104K. A 3-point drop in 
attrition saves $4.7M a year. $6.3M left the building 
chasing career growth opportunities we didn't offer.
These are CFO numbers, not HR numbers.

## Things That Surprised Me

- Small teams (1-3 people) had the highest attrition 
  at 18.11%. I expected the opposite.
- 79.81% of actively disengaged employees are predicted 
  to exit. We already know who they are.
- Sales Reps leaving at 39.76%. Nearly 1 in 2.
  That's not turnover. That's a retention crisis.

## What I Used

Power BI, DAX for the measures and risk scoring logic,
and a lot of time staring at the data model wondering 
why the numbers weren't adding up (they eventually did).

## Honestly, Who Is This For

Three people sitting in the same room.
The CHRO who owns the problem.
The CFO who controls the budget.
The CEO who has to decide.

This isn't a people report.
It's a business case with names on it.
